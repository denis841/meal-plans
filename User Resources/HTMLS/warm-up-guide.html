How to Warm Up

A simple, universal system that works for every exercise.

Why We Warm Up

Raise Temperature Warm muscles perform better and are less injury-prone
Rehearse Movement Your nervous system needs to "practice" before going heavy
Prepare Joints Gradual loading protects tendons and ligaments

The Warm-Up Formula

Use this same system before any compound lift (squats, deadlifts, bench, rows, etc.)

1
General Warm-Up (2-5 minutes) Light cardio until you break a slight sweat. Walking, cycling, rowing, or jumping jacks.
2
Movement Practice (1 set) Empty bar or very light weight. 10-15 slow, controlled reps focusing on form.
3
Ramp-Up Sets (2-3 sets) Gradually increase weight while decreasing reps. Get your body ready for the working weight.
Set Weight Reps How It Feels
1 Light 8 Easy — barely any effort
2 Medium 5 Comfortable — still not challenging
3 Heavy 2-3 Close to working weight — getting ready

Then begin your working sets.

Example: Bench Press (Working at 80kg)
Bar only 20kg 12 reps
Ramp 1 40kg 8 reps
Ramp 2 60kg 5 reps
Ramp 3 70kg 3 reps
Working Sets 80kg 8 reps
Quick Reference — Before Every Compound Lift
  1. General cardio → 2-5 min (break a sweat)
  2. Empty bar/light → 10-15 reps (practice form)
  3. Ramp up gradually → 2-3 sets getting heavier
  4. Working sets → These are tracked in Everfit

Common Questions

Do I need to warm up for every exercise?

Full warm-up for your FIRST compound lift of the session. For subsequent exercises, 1-2 light ramp-up sets is enough since you're already warm.

What about isolation exercises?

Bicep curls, tricep extensions, etc. usually don't need a dedicated warm-up if you've already done compound lifts. One light set is fine.

How long should warm-ups take?

5-10 minutes total. Don't overthink it — the goal is to prepare, not exhaust yourself.

Do warm-up sets count as work?

No. They're preparation. Only your working sets are tracked and progressive.

Prepare the body. Rehearse the movement. Then lift.
